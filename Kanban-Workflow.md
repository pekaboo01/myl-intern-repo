1. How does a Kanban board work, and how does it help manage workflow?

A Kanban board is a visual tool used to manage tasks and track progress. It displays work items as cards that move across different columns representing stages of the workflow. Each column shows the current status of a task, making it easy to see what is being worked on, what is waiting, and what is completed.

Kanban helps manage workflow by making tasks visible to the entire team. This transparency improves communication and helps identify bottlenecks. If tasks are stuck in one column for too long, the team can quickly notice and take action. It supports continuous delivery by allowing work to move steadily instead of waiting for fixed sprint cycles.

2. What do the different columns on a Kanban board represent?

The columns on a Kanban board represent the stages of work. While names may vary by team, common columns include:

Backlog – Tasks that are planned but not yet started.
Not Started – Tasks ready to begin.
In Progress – Tasks currently being worked on.
Blocked – Tasks that cannot move forward due to an issue.
Ready for Review – Tasks completed but waiting for review or approval.
Done – Tasks that are fully completed and approved.

Each column helps track where a task is in the workflow and ensures that nothing is forgotten.

3. How do tasks move through the board, and who is responsible for updating them?

Tasks move from left to right across the board as work progresses. For example, a task begins in the Backlog, moves to In Progress when someone starts working on it, then moves to Review, and finally to Done once completed and approved.

The person responsible for the task is usually responsible for updating its status. This ensures the board reflects real-time progress. Keeping the board updated is important because it allows the team to see accurate information and manage workload effectively.

4. What are the benefits of limiting work in progress (WIP)?

Limiting work in progress means setting a maximum number of tasks that can be in the “In Progress” column at one time. This prevents team members from multitasking too much and becoming overwhelmed.

The benefits include better focus, faster task completion, improved quality, and fewer unfinished tasks. It also helps identify bottlenecks early because if the limit is reached, new tasks cannot start until others are completed.
