1. What is Agile, and how does it differ from traditional project management?

Agile is a way of managing projects and developing software that focuses on flexibility, teamwork, and continuous improvement. Instead of planning everything in detail at the beginning, Agile teams work in small steps and deliver parts of the product regularly. This allows them to adjust quickly if requirements change.

Traditional project management, such as the Waterfall method, follows a strict sequence of steps like planning, designing, building, and testing. Each phase must be completed before moving to the next one. If changes are needed later, it can be difficult and costly. Agile is different because it accepts change as a normal part of the process and focuses on delivering working results continuously instead of waiting until the very end.

2. What are the core values and principles of Agile?

The core values of Agile come from the Agile Manifesto. The manifesto highlights four main values: individuals and interactions over processes and tools, working software over heavy documentation, customer collaboration over contract negotiation, and responding to change over following a fixed plan.

These values show that Agile focuses more on people, teamwork, and delivering real results. There are also twelve principles that guide Agile teams. These include delivering working software frequently, welcoming changing requirements, maintaining a sustainable pace, and continuously improving through reflection. Together, these values and principles help teams stay flexible, productive, and focused on delivering value.

3. What are the differences between Scrum and Kanban, and when is each used?

Scrum and Kanban are two popular Agile frameworks, but they work differently. Scrum uses fixed time periods called sprints, usually lasting one to four weeks. During each sprint, the team plans what tasks they will complete. Scrum also has specific roles like Scrum Master and Product Owner, and it includes regular meetings such as daily stand-ups and sprint reviews. Scrum works best when tasks can be planned ahead and delivered in structured cycles.

Kanban, on the other hand, does not use fixed sprints. It focuses on continuous workflow and visualizing tasks on a board. Tasks can be added at any time, and the team limits how many tasks are in progress to avoid overload. Kanban is more flexible and works well when priorities change often or when work is ongoing, such as bug fixes and support tasks.

4. Why does Focus Bear lean towards Kanban instead of Scrum?

Focus Bear likely leans towards Kanban because its workflow requires flexibility. In QA and automation testing, tasks like debugging, improving scripts, and fixing issues may come unexpectedly. Kanban allows the team to respond quickly without waiting for the next sprint cycle.

Since Kanban focuses on continuous delivery and limiting work in progress, it helps the team stay organized while adapting to changing priorities. This makes it more suitable for an environment where work is ongoing and improvements are made regularly instead of in fixed time periods.
